Research Goal

This research investigates mycelium-based composites as a sustainable alternative to conventional building materials. Mycelium, the root-like network of fungi, can be grown into molds to form lightweight, biodegradable structural panels. The goal is to characterize their mechanical behavior and optimize fabrication parameters to make them viable for real building applications.

The core challenge is understanding how processing conditions affect final material rigidity, since mycelium composites are sensitive to moisture content, heat exposure, and pressing parameters during fabrication.

Testing Methodology

I developed and executed a structured experimental protocol applying heat transfer principles to the heat-press process by systematically varying temperature settings, dwell times, and moisture conditions across controlled trial batches. For each set of parameters, samples were evaluated for Modulus of Rupture (MOR) under mechanical loading to quantify rigidity and failure behavior.

A Python-based ANOVA framework was built to statistically analyze the relationship between process parameters and mechanical outcomes. This model achieved ~95% predictive accuracy in forecasting material behavior under varying moisture conditions, providing a reliable guide for fabrication optimization.

SEM microscopy and optical microscopy were used to characterize the microstructure of samples at various processing stages, revealing how heat and pressure affect the mycelium fiber network at the microscale.

Outcome

The research identified optimal pressing parameters that measurably improved sample rigidity and reproducibility across batches. The findings and fabrication methodology are currently being written up for a peer-reviewed publication, which I am co-authoring alongside the faculty research team.
